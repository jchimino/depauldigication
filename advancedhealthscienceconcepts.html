By JC, written April 20, 2016

This SCD, “is highly visible and generates a significant amount of media and community attention.” The event is plural and often times, affects a youth athlete in the high school or college setting. Rhetoric Chad Asplund uses in the article, The Evidence Against Cardiac Screening Using Electrocardiogram in Athletes, does a wonderful job at minimizing the effects of SCD, just as the article he previously co authored, Cardiac Preparticipation Screening for the Young Athlete . In negating any reason for electrocardiogram (ECG) policy, the title indicates and holds true much evidence against ECG use in today’s sporting organizations. Albeit, the article lags behind their membership’s stance written as the Interassociation Consensus Statement on Cardiovascular Care of College Student Athletes, by Brian Hainline et al.

Precautions for the Active Heart

By JC, written January 29, 2016

    A 1990 headline in USA Today read, “ Athletes at Risk: many put health, even life on the line to pla y.” (McCaffrey, 177) This implies injuries can be sustained from competitive sport as most are well aware of the damage our bodies endure throughout athletic seasons. It also suggests there are risks associated with the unknown, which is why we train and hope our movements and reaction times are quick enough to dodge a punch in boxing, break a tackle in football, avoid a collision in soccer or balance our bodies in gymnastics, but what about when that risk is internal? What about when that risk is the very blood flowing through your veins or the rapid beat of your heart? It is difficult to prepare for or even be aware of those risks. Twentysix years after that infamous headline, I believe science and technology can drastically minimize that internal risk with various precautions.

The Risk of Being a Heart

By JC, written February 22, 2016

    A theory claims your heart is as big as your fist. An unclenched fist shows we all have various hand sizes with long, short, wide, or skinny fingers. As well, our palms range in mass or detail. Of course, our hands grow as we do, as we develop, work, and grow old. So does our heart. A person can learn much from another’s hands and if they’ve heard the theory before, they can guess the size of one’s heart. Certainly with assorted hand sizes, we all have various heart sizes. One key difference though, is if our hands stop working we can hopefully manage to go about our life. If our heart stops working, so does life and so do we. Think again if you consider today’s technology complex. Think twice more if you’ve ever thought a superstar athlete worked harder than any other employee. The single most complex hardest working individual machine is the heart. As such, a computer is turned off at times, an athlete has an off season, both are able to rest and rehabilitate their bodies but the heart doesn’t have this privilege. The heart doesn’t stop, it doesn’t rest ever, or at least until it does, which doesn’t necessarily mean it is capable of ever starting back up again.

Adverse Effects of Energy Supplements

By JC, written January 16, 2016

    It was March Madness 2005, our varsity basketball team was in a regional championship game to advance in the state tournament. We had the home court advantage against a 7’2 Division I committed center on an equally talented opponent. As we watched the first game of the night, prior to getting dressed, stretching and beginning our pregame warm ups, it seemed like the perfect time to consume a Monster “energy drink.” I’ve sparingly done this before prior to school classes, varsity practices or nights out with friends but this time would be my last. By halftime of our game, with 16 minutes of playing time, my face had gone pale, dizziness ensued, tachycardia was apparent and no amount of clothing could have kept me from the chills. The game was eventually won, although I barely remember the details having been disconnected, in a daze, at the end of the bench, unable to compete in the second half.

Concussed Injury

By JC, written May 20, 2016

Concussion Increases Odds of Sustaining a Lower Extremity Musculoskeletal Injury After Return to Play Among Collegiate Athletes by M. Allison Brooks et al. is an article that theoretically aims “to determine the odds of sustaining an acute lower extremity musculoskeletal injury during the 90 day period after return to play from concussion (Brooks, 1).” The authors, which also include athletic trainers, physical therapists and kinesiology students from the University of WisconsinMadison, studied 87 cases of concussed athletes. Those athletes partook in either football, soccer, hockey, softball, basketball, wrestling, or volleyball over a three year span. 182 athletes without concussion in the previous year were then organized as the control group for comparison purposes (Brooks, 1). The results concluded that prevalence of injury to lower limbs was higher within the group of concussed athletes although, “further investigation of neurocognitive and motor control deficits may be warranted beyond the acute injury phase... (Brooks, 1)”

Training Workloads

By JC, written May 2, 2016

    The article titled “ The Training Injury Prevention Paradox: Should Athletes be Training Harder and Smarter?” is very inclusive of many aspects pertaining to workloads and injury prevention. The author, Tim J. Gabbett, offers up many headlines breaking up various sections of content. The information progresses seamlessly as well, beginning with an overview of the relationship between training, injury, fitness and performance through the conclusion, which describes a “vaccine against injury (Gabbett, 7).” In summary, there must be a balance of elements within that relationship. In order to prevent high degrees of risk, internal, external, acute and chronic training loads, monitoring tactics, which are subjective and objective measures and continuous adaptations must be considered.
