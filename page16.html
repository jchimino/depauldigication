An Electrocardio Debate

By Jonathan Chimino

        Chad Asplund and Francis O’Connor write of an, “uncommon” sudden cardiac death (SCD) event. This SCD, “is highly visible and generates a significant amount of media and community attention.” The event is plural and often times, affects a youth athlete in the high school or college setting. Rhetoric Chad Asplund uses in the article, The Evidence Against Cardiac Screening Using Electrocardiogram in Athletes, does a wonderful job at minimizing the effects of SCD, just as the article he previously co authored, Cardiac Preparticipation Screening for the Young Athlete . In negating any reason for electrocardiogram (ECG) policy, the title indicates and holds true much evidence against ECG use in today’s sporting organizations. Albeit, the article lags behind their membership’s stance written as the Interassociation Consensus Statement on Cardiovascular Care of College Student Athletes, by Brian Hainline et al.


        In refuting the need to use electrocardiogram in pre participation screenings, the introduction of Chad Asplund’s article opens by disproving a Veneto, Italian study that witnessed an 89% decrease in SCD within the Veneto region. Their argument became suspicion of heterogenous populations in the US being inherently wired distinctly from Italian homogenous populations, so much as to determine an ECGs ability to have similar effects. The introduction also deciphers between various heart conditions that are more or less detectable by electrocardiogram, such as WolffParkinsonWhite syndrome, Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy and long QT syndrome. The introduction then closes with the automated external defibrillator as a solution in the same sentence the authors acknowledge their peers making progress in the field of ECG interpretation (Asplund, 81).


        An Incidence section is next, presenting statistics that are converted into relevantly low prevalence rates. As rates are minimized, it’s compared and on par with sudden deaths by drug use and suicides. In a transitional paragraph, the author Chimino 2 disputes HCM as a detectable cause prevalent enough for anyone to warrant any widespread use of the ECG (Asplund, 82).


        A foundation is then described for an exemplary screening process. If any process were to be implemented, it should have the ability to influence a disease or health outcome for the public, to be sensitive, specific, acceptable, and to also have high enough prevalence in the population screened. ECG screening is then argued to not meet this criteria, which leads the reader to believe utilizing the ECG technology in preparticipation screening as unnecessary (Asplund, 82). Complications are then attributed to subsequent and additional care or testing. As well, the authors cite an undeveloped infrastructure of untrained specialists that limit screening interpretations. In the same paragraph, the authors acknowledge available online learning resources for the medical industry to utilize (Asplund, 83). These resources could assist in the medical industry reaching that standard of interpretation but only if policy and procedure of ECG screening is implemented, otherwise those resources become wasted. At another point, the author stretches furthest to hint that repercussions from ECG screening such as arterial injury, thrombus formations or arrhythmias may have a more profound negative impact than SCD (Asplund, 83). As well, it’s stated our best chance of survival lies in having rapid access to defibrillation and an emergency action plan (EAP). These precautions are necessary and while they may limit worst case scenarios of SCD, they do not prevent the scenario. At best, automated external defibrillation and EAPs are safety net measures that should be considered a last resort. In their conclusion, Chad Asplund nearly redacts his stance and announces, “to determine these things, we need large scale randomized clinical trials to measure outcomes…” (Asplund, 84).


        As a sport, fitness and recreational leader, one must stay informed on this debate as it most certainly impacts athletic programs and athletes across the spectrum. As a coach or trainer, policy and procedures should be adhered to closely to limit negative outcomes but to also see potential alternatives and drawbacks. Also, physicians like Chad Asplund and Francis O’Connor, while refuting the need for ECG screening, may Chimino 3 indirectly create awareness or more beneficial standards in the process of pointing out faults or inefficiencies. As one may hope such recommended policy is based off a broader consensus, which may not be the case. Even still, it is imperative for one to challenge that consensus to ensure safety and care for our student athletes is not misguided. Knowing the history and progression of the ECG debate allows coaches and trainers that possibility. With student athletes at the forefront of our concerns, in utilizing the article’s information to implement preemptive measures, a sport fitness and recreation leader may also find solutions or better efficiencies unseen by medical staff. In creating the most efficient preparticipation screening process, any point of view or alternative may offer sporting organizations the policy needed to ensure safety and care for the student athlete. While I disagree with this article’s stance, I do agree with their conclusion. At the very least, we must begin to determine these variables with a large scale, randomized clinical trial
