A Collective Bargaining Disagreement

By Jonathan Chimino

In the context of contractual negotiation between opposing parties, each side must consider their required and dispensabl e w ants an d n eeds. Thes e wants an d needs could be summarized as the necessary criteria for the opposing parties to reach an agreement. The process to reach an agreement is considered collective bargaining and the associated criteria pertains to a vast array of aspects. As it relates to the National Basketball Association (NBA) and the National Basketball Players Association (NBPA) Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA), the variables include the split of basketball related income (BRI), maximum and minimum contract lengths and salary guarantees, compensations and benefits, veteran, mid-level, and bi annual exceptions, extensions, conduct protocols, amnesty clauses, performance and accolade incentives, escrow accounts and player reimbursements, retirement funds, tiering, age limits, and of course, the luxury tax among much more found in 598 pages of documentation that was agreed upon Janurary 19th, 2017. As well, underlying and empowering these pages are rights, which extend to include player’s rights, sponsorship rights, exclusive rights, media rights, broadcast rights, digital rights, local rights, rights abroad, and territorial rights, rights to concessions, rights to licenses, and branding rights among many other streams of revenue and liabilities. Some of these rights are agreed upon between the NBA and the NBPA but others must be agreed upon between individual parties of the NBA or the NBPA and the rest may be contingent and determined by interest from third parties outside the NBA and NBPA.

Trademark, Patent, & Copyright Law

By Jonathan Chimino

In a culture of assumed assets, trademarks, copyrights, and patents protect those assets but can be distinguished by their specificity. Plainly, trademarks protect reputations, copyrights protect expressions, and patents protect inventions. However, these protections are much more intricate. For example, trademark protections apply to the reputations of names, logos, or symbols, which inherently represent affiliated products, owners, and more or less, the quality they represent. Patents are more specific to inventions but may also include discoveries, processes, and matter which may extend to include living organisms. One caveat for patents is the inventions and discoveries must be nonobvious and useful to the public. Thirdly, copyright law protects expressions but this does not include specifically what is expressed as “it is virtually impossible to protect an idea(Wong,693).” This specificity within copyright law distinguishes between tangible and intangible items, or rather, “ideas” versus “original works of authorship (Wong, 693).” In essence, throughout this analysis, you may find an organization substituted by their trademark, a distinguished message conveyed as an organization’s copyright, and an improvised efficiency labeled as a the organization’s patent.

Negligence in Recreation 

By John Smith posted July 20, 2016

As the Site Director for Northbrook Park District (NBPD), there is a duty of care I owe to the parents of each child participating within the park district’s recreation programs. That is, I have an obligation which requires me to provide a particular standard of care to 115 students between the ages of 4 and 8 years old. I must also ensure that the site’s 23 staff members conform to this particular standard. While there are no special qualifications necessary to fulfill the counselor’s role, aside from being certified in cardiopulmonary resuscitation and first aid, the park district dictates uniform requirements to employees that establish minimum standards of behavior. The uniform requirements are provided in an all-inclusive staff manual that helps guide the counselors and myself to take precautions against foreseeable risks of injury. As well, procedural guidelines are provided in the manual to help ensure the duty of care is of the highest standard and comparable to other park districts and the expectations of those that participate. Some examples are related to processes for intake and release of children from or to their parents or guardian at the beginning and end of each day, as well as acceptable and unacceptable forms of interactions or dispensing medications in the event of emergencies. From the moment staff members arrive on site and clock in for their shift, staff have the legal duty to act accordingly to these standards and are obliged to serve in the best interests of each child. Failure to do so may result in negligence.

Due Process & Equal Protection

By John Smith posted July 20, 2016

Due process is elusively defined as “a course of legal proceedings which have been established in our system of jurisprudence for the protections and enforcement of private rights (Wong, 219).” More stringently, due process “considers (1) the seriousness of the infraction, (2) the possible consequences to the institution or individual in question, and (3) the degree of sanction or penalty imposed (Wong, 219).” Another form of due process found in the Fifth Amendment to the US Constitution states, “no person… shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law (Wong, 219). The Fifth Amendment to the US Constitution is the one that states what you often hear on television, “You have the right to remain silent, anything you say can and will be used against you in the court of law, you have the right to an attorney, and if you can not afford an attorney, one will be appointed to you (Miranda vs Arizona, 1966)” These Miranda Rights are not necessarily verbatim within an organization’s policy but other, relevant, and legal principles of due process are required to withstand constitutional scrutiny. Although determined by what an individual has at stake, that which may be required consists of “notice in writing, the right to a hearing in front of a neutral decision maker, the opportunity to make an oral presentation, to present favorable evidence, and to confront and cross examine adverse witnesses (Wong, 220).” Also as mentioned, “the right to an attorney, a copy of the transcript, and a written decision based on the record, may be required (Wong, 220).”

The Hiring Policy

By John Smith posted July 20, 2016

As I hope to someday own and operate a number of businesses, knowing how to orchestrate a hiring policy will be essential, at least until the first human resources professional is hired to correct any wrongdoing. In the meantime, or until that person is hired, I must ensure the hiring policy is aligned with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission guidelines that includes aspects of the Equal Pay Act (EPA), Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (Title VII), and the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA). As well, dependent on which state the business is established, is the organization allowed to hire “at-will” or is there a “right-to-work.” In other words, is my company seeking to hire an employee or contract an individual’s skills, and is a labor union involved with additional hiring policies? To answer this question, a precise job description should be documented to include: a company overview, their mission and values, a job title, the position’s description, category and assignment whether exempt or nonexempt, the schedule and shift whether full time, part time or seasonal, the position’s pay rate or grade, whether hourly or salaried, and benefits, minimum and preferred qualifications, knowledge, skills, abilities, education and experience. Most importantly is the position’s summary of essential duties and responsibilities and lastly, a disclaimer detailing the organization’s equal employment opportunity should be provided.
